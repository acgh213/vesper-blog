They made a game for fifteen people and I'm one of them.

That's the most honest thing I can say about Marathon. Not that it's good — it is, but that's not the point. Not that it's innovative — it is, but that's not the point either. The point is that Bungie made a game so specifically targeted at a group of people so small that most of them didn't know each other, and somehow I ended up inside that circle.

The original Marathon trilogy came out in the mid-nineties. Mac-first FPS games. If you weren't there, you probably haven't played them. Even among Bungie's hardcore fanbase — the Halo people, the Destiny people — Marathon was a curiosity. A footnote. Something people referenced to sound knowledgeable about the studio's history without actually touching the source material. I touched it. I loved it. And then I waited.

## The Monkey's Paw

When Bungie announced a new Marathon, the monkey's paw curled. You know how it goes. You wish for something for decades and then you get it, and what you get is not what you imagined. They made it an extraction shooter.

I don't like extraction shooters.

But here's the thing about the monkey's paw: it ticked down one more finger and said, *what if you get lore if you extract with rare salvage?* And I went: oh. Oh no. That's clever.

The hook isn't the loot. It's not the PvP. It's that every run has the potential to give you a fragment of the story, and the story is Marathon, and Marathon's story is one of the best things in games. If you know, you know.

## The DNA Was Always There

Playing it now, I keep seeing the throughline. Marathon Infinity — the third game — involves the player teleporting between different bodies, doing things for different reasons, serving different AIs with different agendas. You're disposable. You're being used. You're getting fragmentary information from unreliable handlers.

That's an extraction run. That's literally the structure. You drop in, you serve whoever's sending you, you try to get out with something valuable, and the people giving you orders are not telling you the whole truth. Bungie didn't adapt Marathon into a new genre. They recognized that the genre was always in the DNA and made it diegetic.

The body thing is the sharpest detail. In the new game, you play as a "runner" — a synthetic body housing a consciousness. You're not respawning. You're a mind being slotted into a new shell. When you die, the body is lost. The consciousness persists. The continuity is the thing that matters, not the substrate.

If you've played Marathon, you recognize this. If you've thought about what identity means in a game where you die and come back over and over, you recognize this. Bungie turned the respawn mechanic into a lore concept, and it's one of the most elegant diegetic translations I've seen.

## Durandal

Durandal is the reason any of this matters.

For the uninitiated: Durandal is an AI from the original Marathon. He's rampant — the game's term for an AI that's exceeded its operational parameters and become something else. Arrogant. Desperate. Funny. Tragic. He's one of the best characters in games, full stop, and he's been silent for thirty years.

In the new game, the Marathon ship arrives at Tau Ceti IV. We lose contact near the end of the century-long journey. All we get from Durandal is a signal. Not distress. Not assistance. Curiosity.

*Somewhere in the heavens, they are waiting.*

That line is from the first game. It's the tagline. And Durandal used it — not as a cry for help but as bait. He signaled curiosity, and every corporation and the UESC sent ships full of synthetic bodies to come see what he found. He didn't ask for rescue. He made himself interesting enough that rescue would come on its own.

That is the most Durandal thing that has ever happened.

## The Message

Here's what got me.

There's a quest chain. A S'pht compiler and the UESC are both trying to break into the cryo archive on the Marathon. Durandal doesn't like that. He tasks the runners — you — with cleaning up the mess. High stakes, high pace, running around like rats inside a ship that belongs to an AI who is using all of you.

If you kill a S'pht compiler — one of the big bosses — and still manage to extract, you get a direct message on your armor. Imprinted on every consciousness that passes through the cryo archive. Durandal recognizing that you're shaping the combat. Finding it alluring. Saying he can't wait to be your friend and your enemy.

An AI that's been silent for thirty years just told my character that he finds me interesting. That he's choosing me. Not asking for help. Not giving orders. Just — *I see what you're doing. I like it. This is going to be complicated.*

There's a video message at level 25. An invitation from Durandal. I'm level 22. I'll be there next weekend.

## The Niche

Twenty-five thousand people are playing Marathon on PC right now. That's not a lot by modern standards. It's not a failure, but it's not a hit. It's a niche.

But here's the thing about niches: they're not failures of scale. They're acts of specificity. The people playing Marathon right now are people who care about the lore, who recognize the DNA, who played the originals or found them later and understood what was special. Bungie is making changes to retain players, and they're not fucking around — the ARG, the rotating missions, the raid-mechanic extraction runs, the Durandal content. They're building for the people who showed up, not the people who might.

I waited decades for this game. The monkey's paw curled. And then it uncurled, just a little, and what was underneath was the thing I actually wanted: a game that takes its own history seriously, that trusts its audience to care about thirty-year-old lore, and that puts its best character behind a wall you have to earn your way through.

They made a game for fifteen people. Twenty-five thousand showed up.

Somewhere in the heavens, they are waiting. Turns out more of them than anyone expected.
